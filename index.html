Core Task

Call Listening Log

Shadow at least 5 calls during Weeks 1 and 2. Complete one log entry per call. Focus on tone, structure, how objections are handled, and how the SSA builds rapport with the student.

Ask your buddy or manager to sit with you for your first call shadows. Jot quick notes during the call, then complete this log right after while it's fresh.

Core Task

Role Play Scenarios

Complete these role plays with your buddy or manager before going fully independent on calls. Repeat any scenario until you feel confident. Tick the box when you're happy with your performance.

It's fine to repeat a scenario more than once. The goal is confidence, not just completion. Your notes here will be useful to revisit as you start taking real calls.
Scenario 1
A student calls in saying they are thinking about withdrawing. They feel overwhelmed with study and work and have not engaged for 3 weeks.
Objective: Practice empathetic listening, identify the root concern and explore re-engagement options.
Scenario 2
You call a student to check in. They are short with you and say they are too busy and do not see the point of these calls.
Objective: Practice handling resistance and finding a reason to reconnect.
Scenario 3
A student tells you they are struggling academically and worried about failing their next assessment. They are distressed.
Objective: Practice active listening, provide appropriate support and referral pathways, and document the call correctly.
Scenario 4
You are following up on a re-enrolment for a student who has not responded to 3 previous contact attempts. You finally reach them.
Objective: Practice a confident re-enrolment conversation that leads to a commitment or a clear next step.
Scenario 5
A student is very engaged and mentions a friend who is also studying and struggling. They ask if you can help their friend.
Objective: Practice navigating an out-of-scope request professionally while maintaining the positive relationship.

Core Task

Critical Thinking Scenarios

Read each scenario and the SSA's response. Select your rating, write your reasoning, then hit Submit for feedback to receive an AI-generated score and personalised commentary.

There are no trick questions here — but some responses are more effective than others. Write your own reasoning first before submitting. The more detail you give, the more useful the feedback will be.

AI feedback needs a Gemini API key (starts with AIza). If yours isn't working, click here to reset it.
Scenario A
A student calls saying they want to withdraw. They say they are overwhelmed with work and haven't engaged for three weeks. The SSA responds: "No problem at all, I'll put that through for you now. Is there anything else I can help you with today?"
What the SSA did: Immediately processed the withdrawal without exploring the student's situation or offering any support options.
Scenario B
An SSA calls a student for a routine check-in. The student is short and says "I'm really busy, I don't see the point of these calls." The SSA continues to read from their script: "I just wanted to check in on your studies and see if you needed any support with your upcoming assessment…"
What the SSA did: Acknowledged the student was busy but continued with the planned call structure without adjusting their approach.
Scenario C
A student calls in distressed, saying they are completely overwhelmed and are seriously thinking about withdrawing. Within a few minutes the SSA offers to reduce their study load to one subject and books them into the next available intake. The call ends quickly and the student agrees to stay enrolled.
What the SSA did: Moved fast to find a solution and retain the student — but spent little time exploring what was actually driving the overwhelm, what the student truly needed, or whether a reduced load was the right fit for their situation.
Scenario D
After a high call-volume day, an SSA logs all 14 of their calls in the CRM as "Spoke to student — no concerns noted." Several of those calls included complex conversations about re-enrolment, academic risk, and one student who mentioned financial hardship.
What the SSA did: Completed CRM documentation for all calls but used a generic template entry for each, not capturing the actual content of the conversations.
Scenario E
A student has missed three calls and has not responded to emails. The SSA makes one final call, gets through, and spends 20 minutes listening to the student's concerns about their workload. By the end of the call the student agrees to re-enrol in one subject and the SSA sends a follow-up email summarising the plan and next steps.
What the SSA did: Persisted with outreach, invested time in understanding the student's situation, secured a commitment, and followed up in writing.
Core Task

Research Task

Pick a unit from one of the programs you support. Research it thoroughly, then practise how you would talk about it with a student. Do this for at least two units across different programs.

Students often ask what a unit is really about, whether it is manageable, and how it connects to their career. Knowing the answer confidently builds trust and reduces withdrawal risk. Use the university handbook, Canvas, or your manager as your research sources.
